Mercury

The Swift Planet

Overview

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the Solar System. It's also the fastest, orbiting the Sun in just 88 Earth days (Thats why we say "The Swift Planet").

This rocky world has virtually no atmosphere to retain heat, resulting in extreme temperature variations from scorching hot during the day to freezing cold at night.

  • Distance from Sun: 22-43 million miles (0.24-0.47 AU)
  • Diameter: 3,032 miles (4,879 kilometers)
  • Length of Day: 59 Earth days
  • Length of Year: 88 Earth days
  • Surface Temperature: -280°F to 800°F (-173°C to 427°C)

Mercury as imaged by NASA's MESSENGER spacecraft, showing its cratered surface.

Mercury's Atmosphere

Mercury doesn't have a real atmosphere like Earth. Instead, it has what scientists call an exosphere, which is an extremely thin layer of gases.

Note: We haven't included an image of Mercury's exosphere because we couldn't find one from a reliable scientific source, good pictures are limited (A future update might bring this!)

Key Characteristics:

  • Composition: Mostly oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, helium, and some potassium atoms.
  • Pressure: Almost none, about one trillionth of Earth's atmospheric pressure.
  • Temperature Range: Extreme changes from 800°F (430°C) during the day to -290°F (-180°C) at night.
  • Notable Features: The exosphere is constantly changing as atoms escape to space and new ones are added from the surface of mercury.
  • Interactions: Solar wind and small meteorites hitting Mercury's surface kick up atoms that become part of the exosphere.

Without a real atmosphere, there's nothing to trap heat on Mercury, which is why temperatures change so drastically between day and night!

Internal Structure & Magnetic Field

Mercury is built differently than other planets. It has a huge metal core that takes up most of the planet!

Mercury's Layers:

  • Core: A massive iron core that makes up about 85% of Mercury's size. The outer part is liquid metal while the inner part is solid.
  • Mantle: A thin rock like layer around the core, that is only about 310-435 miles thick.
  • Crust: The thin outer layer of rock that we would stand on, only about 100-300 km thick with many cracks and ridges.

Mercury has a magnetic field like Earth, but much weaker, only about 1% as strong. Scientists say the moving liquid metal in Mercury's outer core creates this magnetic field, like a massive magnet inside the planet!

The MESSENGER Mission

MESSENGER spacecraft being assembled before its launch to Mercury.

Mercury Surface, Space Environment, Geochemistry, and Ranging (MESSENGER) was NASA's first mission to orbit Mercury, revealing groundbreaking details about the innermost planet.

Mission Highlights:

  • Launch Date: August 3, 2004
  • Mercury Arrival: March 18, 2011
  • End of Mission: April 30, 2015 (crashed into Mercury's surface)
  • Mapping Coverage: 100% of Mercury's surface
  • Key Discoveries: Confirmed water/ice at Mercury's poles, discovered high concentrations of magnesium and calcium on the surface, as well as mapping the planet's magnetic field

After completing its objectives, MESSENGER continued to orbit Mercury for an extended mission, gathering around 277,000 images and a wealth of scientific data before running out of fuel.

Phases of Mercury

Mercury, like the Moon, displays different phases as it orbits the Sun.

Mercury shows phases similar to Venus/Moon because of its position compared to Earth and the Sun. These phases occur as our viewing angle of Mercury's bright hemisphere changes during its orbit.

Surface of Mercury

Notable Surface Features:

  • Craters: Mercury's surface is heavily cratered like the Moon (result of no atmosphere), with Picasso crater being one of its most unique impact features.
  • Fun Facts: Although Mercury looks a bit dead now, it used to have widespread volcanic activity, with ancient lava flows covering around 6% of its surface. Evidence suggests volcanic eruptions ended about 3.5 billion years ago.
  • Plains: Mercury has smooth plains areas, particularly inside craters, that were formed by ancient volcanic lava flows filling impact basins.
  • Poles: Despite being the closest to the Sun, Mercury is permanently dark at its poles where temperatures never rise above -280°F (-173°C), allowing water/ice to exist even on this scorching planet.
  • Major Impact Features: The Caloris Basin is Mercury's most notable impact feature, created by an asteroid collision early in the planet's history.
