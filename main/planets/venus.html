Venus

The Hottest Planet in Our Solar System

Basics

Venus is the second planet from the Sun, and it is also the closest planet to Earth. It's one of the four terrestrial planets.

It has a toxic atmosphere filled with carbon dioxide and clouds made of sulfuric acid. The atmosphere traps heat in a runaway greenhouse effect, which is why Venus is the hottest planet in our solar system.

  • Diameter: 7,521 miles

Atmosphere

Picture of Venus' middle and lower atmospheres.

Venus has a thick, toxic atmosphere filled with carbon dioxide and clouds of sulfric acid.

Key Characteristics:

  • Chemical Composition: 96% carbon dioxide, 3.5% nitrogen, with small amounts of sulfur dioxide andd other gaseses
  • Greenhouse Effect: Venus experiences a runaway greenhouse effect that traps heat, making it a very hot planet.
  • Cloud Layers: There are three main cloud layers at 28-43 miles altitude
  • Acid Rain: There is Acid Rain on Venus. This acid rain evaporates before reaching the surface due to the extreme heat

The thick atmosphere creates a strong greenhouse effect, trapping heat and making Venus the hottest planet in our solar system despite being farther from the Sun than Mercury.

Internal Structure

Venus's internal structure is believed to be similar to Earth's, with a core, mantle, and crust.

Venus's Layers:

  • Core: Likely a partially liquid iron core approximately 1,850 miles in radius
  • Mantle: A rocky mantle composed primarily of silicate materials
  • Crust: A rigid crust estimated to be 30 miles thick on average

Venus lacks a magnetic field, suggesting its core may not be generating the same kind of dynamo effect as Earth's. This absence of a magnetic field means solar radiation interacts directly with the upper atmosphere.

The Magellan Mission

The Magellan spacecraft in the Kennedy Space Center.

NASA's Magellan mission was a probe sent to get data of Venus. Launched in 1989, it arrived to Venus in 1990 and spent four years mapping the surface.

Mission Highlights:

  • Launch Date: May 4, 1989
  • Venus Arrival: August 10, 1990
  • End of Mission: October 13, 1994
  • Coverage: 98% of Venus's surface
  • Resolution: Down to 300 feet

The Magellan mission helped us understand Venus more by letting us discover valcanoes, vast lava plains, and unique tectonic features. The data gave us the most detailed global map of Venus.

Surface of Venus

Venus has a dangerous surface. The surface has many dangerous features, including vast plains formed by lava flows, many large volcanoes, and unique features not found anywhere else in the solar system.
