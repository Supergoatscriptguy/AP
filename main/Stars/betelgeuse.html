Betelgeuse

The Red Supergiant of Orion

Overview

Betelgeuse (Alpha Orionis) is one of the largest and most luminous stars visible to the naked eye. Located in the constellation Orion, it forms the distinctive reddish "shoulder" of the hunter in the night sky. As a red supergiant star, Betelgeuse is in the late stages of its stellar evolution.

It's a variable star, meaning its brightness changes over time. In late 2019, Betelgeuse underwent an unexpected and dramatic dimming, leading some to speculate it might be about to go supernova, though it later returned to its normal brightness.

  • Classification: Red Supergiant (M1-M2)
  • Distance from Earth: ~548 light-years
  • Diameter: ~887 million km (617× Sun's diameter)
  • Mass: 11-12 solar masses
  • Luminosity: ~126,000 times the Sun
  • Surface Temperature: ~3,500 K

If Betelgeuse were placed at the center of our solar system, its surface would extend beyond the orbit of Jupiter, engulfing Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, and the asteroid belt.

Life Cycle and Future

Artist's impression of what Betelgeuse might look like when it explodes as a supernova.

Betelgeuse has already lived through the main part of its life cycle and is now in its final stages. As a massive star, it burned through its hydrogen much faster than our Sun.

Stellar Evolution:

  • Birth: Formed from a giant molecular cloud about 8-8.5 million years ago
  • Main Sequence: Spent about 8 million years as a hot, blue O-type star
  • Red Supergiant Phase: Currently in this phase, which began roughly 40,000 years ago
  • Future Supernova: Expected to explode as a Type II supernova within the next 100,000 years

When Betelgeuse goes supernova, it will be one of the most spectacular astronomical events visible from Earth in human history. It would shine as brightly as a half-moon for weeks and be visible during daytime. Despite its close proximity to us, the supernova poses no threat to Earth.

After the supernova, Betelgeuse will likely leave behind a neutron star or possibly a black hole, depending on the exact mass remaining after the explosion.

Surface and Atmosphere

Unlike our Sun with its well-defined surface, Betelgeuse has a complex, extended atmosphere that gradually thins out into space. Its surface features enormous convection cells that cause the star to pulsate and contribute to its variable brightness.

Remarkable Features:

  • Enormous Convection Cells: These "star spots" are so large that each one would dwarf our entire Sun
  • Mass Loss: Betelgeuse is losing mass rapidly, shedding about one solar mass every 10,000 years
  • Dust Shell: The star is surrounded by multiple shells of ejected material extending over 400 AU (Astronomical Units)
  • Stellar Wind: Powerful outflows travel at about 17 km/s, much slower than our Sun's solar wind

The 2019-2020 "Great Dimming" of Betelgeuse puzzled astronomers until they discovered it was caused by a large dust cloud ejected from the star, temporarily blocking some of its light. This event provided valuable insights into the star's behavior and mass-loss mechanisms.

Observation History

Betelgeuse has been observed by humans for thousands of years due to its brightness and distinctive reddish color. Its name derives from the Arabic "yad al-jauzā" meaning "the hand of Orion," though it was corrupted through translations over centuries.

Key Observations:

  • Ancient Observations: Mentioned in ancient texts from many cultures, including Chinese astronomical records dating back to 100 BCE
  • First Diameter Measurement: In 1920, Albert Michelson and Francis Pease made the first measurement of a star's diameter using the Michelson interferometer
  • HST Imaging (1995): The Hubble Space Telescope captured the first direct image of Betelgeuse's surface, revealing its asymmetric shape
  • ALMA Observations (2017): Provided detailed temperature measurements of the star's atmosphere
  • "Great Dimming" (2019-2020): Observed by both amateur and professional astronomers when the star dimmed to its lowest recorded brightness

Modern observations using interferometry and adaptive optics have revealed that Betelgeuse has a complex, asymmetric shape that changes over time, further evidence of its dynamic nature and ongoing mass loss.

Cultural Significance

The Orion constellation as seen from Earth, with Betelgeuse marked as the bright reddish star at the upper left.

As one of the brightest and most distinctive stars in the night sky, Betelgeuse has played a significant role in various cultures throughout human history.

Cultural References:

  • Ancient Egypt: Associated with the god Osiris
  • Indigenous Australian Astronomy: Featured in various Dreamtime stories
  • Hawaiian Culture: Known as Kaulua-koko, associated with navigational knowledge
  • Modern Pop Culture: The titular character in the film "Beetlejuice" (1988) was named after the star
  • Science Fiction: Mentioned in numerous sci-fi works, including "The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy"

The star's name has been spelled many different ways throughout history: Betelgeuse, Betelgeux, Beteigeuze, and even "Beetlejuice." Its distinctive red color and position in the easily recognizable Orion constellation have made it one of the most culturally significant stars in the night sky.
